a vision journey

2004
Sector & policy25 Jun 2004

WHO global estimate: 161 million visually impaired

In 2004 the WHO's global estimate stood at 161 million people visually impaired, 37 million of them blind - figures based on best-corrected acuity, so anyone whose sight could be fixed with a pair of glasses went uncounted. Uncorrected refractive error, the biggest single cause of poor vision, was missing from the world's numbers.

Adlens17 Nov 2004

Adlens founded

Adlens was incorporated in Oxford in November 2004, built around adjustable-lens spectacles - the technology that would draw James into the vision sector.

2005
Adlens1 Apr 2005

James joins Adlens as director

James joined Adlens as a director in April 2005, alongside the Oxford physicist Joshua Silver. What engaged him was the gap between the sector's numbers and what he could see: far more people needed a pair of glasses than had an eye disease, and the obstacle was delivery rather than technology.

2006
Adaptive Eyewear11 Aug 2006

Adaptive Eyewear founded

In 2006 James founded Adaptive Eyewear, a not-for-profit venture to take adjustable glasses - spectacles the wearer can focus themselves - to places with almost no eye care at all. Poor vision barely registered as a global development issue at the time; James had decided to treat it as one.

2007
Sector & policy16 Nov 2007

WHO's first estimate of uncorrected refractive error

In late 2007 the WHO published its first estimate of uncorrected refractive error: 153 million people. Counting them at all was a milestone, but James believed, from his own experience of glasses in the developing world, that the true number - concentrated in low- and middle-income countries - was far larger.

2009
Adaptive Eyewear7 Aug 2009

Rwanda Phase 1A pilot (Adaptive Eyewear)

Adaptive Eyewear's first major project put the idea into practice. Working with Rwanda's Ministry of Health in seven villages in Gicumbi District, the team tested whether community health workers given basic training in vision screening could assess vision and dispense glasses. Over ten days more than a thousand people were examined, and the model held up.

2010
Adaptive EyewearDate TBC

Meeting with the World Bank

With the pilot results in hand, James approached the World Bank about a partnership, and was turned down. It was an understandable decision at the time: the WHO had only recently begun counting uncorrected refractive error, and there was no evidence that glasses mattered alongside priorities like malaria. The evidence James needed - reaching beyond health into income and productivity - did not yet exist, and creating it became a defining part of the work that followed.

Adaptive Eyewear3 Jun 2010

MOU with Rwanda Ministry of Health

Rwanda was willing where Washington was not. In June 2010 the Ministry of Health signed an agreement with Adaptive Eyewear for a national vision screening programme - one that already carried, in the document itself, the name Vision for a Nation.

Adaptive Eyewear5 Jul 2010

Rwanda Phase 1B piloting begins (Adaptive Eyewear)

A month later, fieldwork began on the next phase: scaling the pilot from seven villages towards the whole of Gicumbi District and its 439,000 people.

VFAN17 Nov 2010

Vision for a Nation (VFAN) founded

By the end of the year the programme name had become an organisation. Vision for a Nation was founded in London to take on what Adaptive Eyewear had started: delivering eye care to the whole of Rwanda.

2012
VFAN26 Mar 2012

New MoU between VFAN and Rwanda Ministry of Health

In March 2012 Rwanda's government and the foundation signed the agreement that underpinned a genuinely national programme. It was designed from the outset to be handed over - the explicit goal was a service the Rwandan government would ultimately own and run itself.

VFAN7 Nov 2012

Full national programme launches in Rwanda

The rollout began in Gicumbi in November 2012 and moved district by district across the country, with nurses trained in every health centre and affordable glasses on their shelves, and James driving it throughout.

2013
Adaptive EyewearDate TBC

Attempt to bring adjustables to IAPB

James also took the adjustable lens to the International Agency for the Prevention of Blindness, the sector's umbrella body, and found an organisation still centred on eye disease and surgery, with little room for an argument about glasses. He carried on regardless.

VFAN21 May 2013

MoU extension signed with Ministry of Health

In 2013 the agreement with Rwanda was extended and sharpened, with universal primary eye care coverage now written into its targets.

2015
VFAN21 Sep 2015

New MoU with Ministry of Health (2015-2017)

By 2015 the work had gone well enough for Rwanda to commit, in a new agreement, to take full ownership of its eye care service from the start of 2018 - the handover the partnership had been designed around - while James backed the final push to reach all 15,000 villages.

Clearly & advocacy24 Sep 2015

Project Oversight begins - New York Times advertisement

In September 2015 James took the argument to a wider audience. The day before the UN adopted seventeen Sustainable Development Goals with no mention of vision, he placed an advertisement in The New York Times calling the omission what it was: an oversight, affecting 2.5 billion people. It was the beginning of a campaign that would occupy him for the next six years.

Sector & policy25 Sep 2015

SDGs launched at UNGA - vision omitted

The 2030 Agenda was adopted with vision absent from every goal and target. For James it summed up the problem: an issue counted in millions had been easy to leave out.

Clearly & advocacy8 Oct 2015

Project Oversight launch event, New York (World Sight Day)

On World Sight Day James launched Project Oversight at a reception in New York, announcing seed grants for ideas to reimagine access to vision correction. The initiative would shortly grow into something larger.

Clearly & advocacyOct 2015 · approx.

James commits to fund the campaign that becomes Clearly

That same month James made the decision that shaped the years ahead: he committed to back a global campaign to put vision correction on the world's agenda, bringing his energy, expertise and network to it. It became Clearly.

Launch of Clearly

The campaign went public on 6 April 2016, launching from Hong Kong with the 2.5 billion figure in the opening line of its press release; James spent the morning of the launch making the case on radio and television. The name stated the goal: to help the world to see clearly.

The Rwanda work was recognised at the UK Charity Awards, taking the top prize for international aid and development - external validation that the model was working.

Clearly's first major initiative was a global prize for innovations in access to vision correction, which drew more than a hundred entries from twenty-one countries. Vula Mobile took the inaugural award.

2017
Clearly & advocacy4 Apr 2017

Clearly 2035 summit, Murano, Venice

In April 2017 James convened a summit on Murano, the island where spectacles were first made seven centuries earlier. Alongside the eye care sector he brought his own network of influential figures from politics, campaigning, business and communications - people the sector rarely reached - and the discussions produced the campaign's central idea: vision as a golden thread running through the Sustainable Development Goals, a development issue rather than a narrow health one.

Clearly & advocacy20 Sep 2017

Precursor meeting with UN members

During UNGA week, James and his team held early conversations at the United Nations - the beginnings of what became the UN's first working group on vision.

Clearly & advocacy12 Oct 2017

Clearly - the book published

In October 2017 James published Clearly, setting out the campaign's case and its plan. It was written with the following spring in mind: something to put into the hands of the Commonwealth's leaders.

2018
VFAN1 Jan 2018

VFAN Rwanda completion - handover to Ministry of Health

On 1 January 2018, as agreed three years earlier, Rwanda's government took full ownership of its national eye care service - the first low-income country to offer its whole population access to vision correction. An eye health module in the national nurse training curriculum meant the expertise would renew itself with each new cohort.

Clearly & advocacy17 Apr 2018

CHOGM, London

The book did its work. Following a letter to Commonwealth leaders in May 2017, James's team made their case at the 2018 Heads of Government Meeting in London, and all 53 leaders committed to 'quality eye care for all' - the first formal recognition of poor vision at a gathering of world leaders.

Clearly & advocacy17 Apr 2018

White-rimmed glasses at Global Citizen, Brixton

That evening the campaign took a lighter form: James on stage at a Global Citizen concert in Brixton in oversized white-rimmed glasses, with Prime Minister Gaston Browne of Antigua & Barbuda and President Mokgweetsi Masisi of Botswana wearing the same.

VFAN15 May 2018

MoU with Ministry of Health (2018-2020)

The Rwanda partnership continued past the handover, with a further agreement covering school screening in the Western Province.

Research & evidence15 May 2018

Rothschild cost analysis of the Rwanda programme

The programme's economics were assessed independently by the Fondation Ophtalmologique A. de Rothschild, which found it cost-effective by the WHO's benchmark and affordable for the government to sustain. Together with the clinical evaluation, the case to other countries was complete: the model works, and it can be afforded.

Research & evidence23 Jul 2018

PROSPER I published in The Lancet Global Health

PROSPER answered the question the World Bank had asked years earlier. The first randomised controlled trial of its kind found that giving Indian tea pickers an affordable pair of reading glasses raised their productivity by more than a fifth - evidence that correcting vision is an economic intervention as well as a medical one. Published in The Lancet Global Health and covered by The Economist and the BBC, it became central to the campaign's case at the UN.

Clearly & advocacy11 Oct 2018

UN Friends of Vision group convened

The conversations begun at the UN in September 2017 led, a year later, to the first meeting of the UN Friends of Vision on World Sight Day 2018 - convened by Ambassador Aubrey Webson of Antigua & Barbuda, with diplomats from ten countries taking part.

Research & evidence7 Dec 2018

Rwanda PEC process evaluation published

Rather than assess its own work, VFAN commissioned an independent academic evaluation of the Rwanda programme. Published in BMC Health Services Research, it found the care delivered matched the design, with over ninety per cent of observed examinations performed correctly.

Clearly filled London's Science Museum for Sightgeist, with June Sarpong hosting, Brian Cox giving the keynote, a DFID minister announcing new funding from the stage - and nine-year-old Lowri Moore asking Disney why its princesses never wear glasses. VFAN19 Jun 2019

VFAN Ghana programme in the field

The model moved to a second country. By mid-2019 Vision for a Nation was on the ground in Ghana, working with the Ghana Health Service to test whether the Rwanda approach could be repeated.

Moonshot philanthropy25 Jun 2019

James debuts 'privatise failure, socialise success' at AVPN, Singapore

Speaking at the Asian Venture Philanthropy Network conference in Singapore, James set out the philosophy behind his approach in a phrase that stuck: philanthropists should privatise failure and socialise success, taking on the risks that institutions cannot. Research & evidence15 Sep 2019

DRIVE research programme begins (SWISH trial)

James put the evidence work on a systematic footing with DRIVE, a portfolio of trials across education, road safety, income and wellbeing, designed to establish what vision correction changes in each of those domains.

Sector & policy8 Oct 2019

WHO World Report on Vision

In October 2019 the WHO published its first World Report on Vision, putting the global figure at 2.2 billion people - counting, for the first time, the uncorrected refractive error James had spent years arguing about. The sector's numbers had moved from millions to billions.

Clearly & advocacy21 Nov 2019

World Report on Vision comes to UN headquarters

A month later the report was launched at UN Headquarters, hosted by Ambassador Webson and the WHO, with Dr Binagwaho on the panel and the President of the General Assembly receiving a copy. Two days after that, Friends of Vision ran eye tests in the UN Secretariat lobby.

The year closed with an honorary doctorate from the University of Bath, recognising the vision work and Rwanda in particular.

2021
Clearly & advocacy4 Jan 2021

Clearly merges with IAPB

Clearly's campaigning had proved effective enough that the sector's peak body wanted it in-house. The campaign merged into the IAPB in January 2021, with James continuing as Global Ambassador, and its machinery for reaching policymakers and governments now worked from inside the sector, at scale.

Sector & policy16 Feb 2021

Lancet Global Health Commission on Global Eye Health

The Lancet Global Health Commission on Global Eye Health confirmed uncorrected refractive error as the leading cause of vision impairment worldwide and set out the evidence for eye health as a development issue - the golden thread argument, now in the field's most authoritative pages.

Sector & policy27 May 2021

World Health Assembly endorses first global refractive error target

Then the world's health ministers made it policy. The World Health Assembly adopted the first-ever global targets for eye care, and refractive error was at their heart: a 40-percentage-point increase in effective coverage by 2030. The issue that wasn't even counted twenty years earlier now had its own global target.

Moonshot philanthropy15 Jun 2021

James launches 'moonshot philanthropy'

With the campaign's methods being vindicated at the UN and the WHO, James gave the philosophy behind them a name: moonshot philanthropy - the argument that owners of capital are free to take risks institutions cannot, privatising failure and socialising success. What had been his own practice for years became a framework offered to other philanthropists.

Clearly & advocacy23 Jul 2021

UN Resolution on vision

On 23 July 2021 all 193 member states of the United Nations unanimously adopted the first resolution on vision - 'Vision for Everyone' - committing the world to eye care for all by 2030. Times Square carried the news that night. Six years after a newspaper advertisement pointed out an oversight, the issue had the UN's unanimous voice behind it.

Research & evidence1 Sep 2021

ENGINE research project launched

Years of campaigning - and the PROSPER result above all - had changed the way the scientific community viewed refractive error. The measure of that shift: the Wellcome Trust, one of the world's great research funders, awarded over £3 million for ENGINE, a five-year suite of global trials on vision and the Sustainable Development Goals. The question James had once struggled to get taken seriously was now a mainstream research priority.

Sector & policySep 2021 · approx.

IAPB launches 2030 In Sight strategy

A quiet high point came with the sector's new strategy, 2030 In Sight: for the first time it spoke not only of blindness and eye disease, but of vision in all its facets - as something to elevate, integrate and activate across health, education and economies. It was the very shift in worldview James had been campaigning for since the World Bank showed him the door.

2025
Moonshot philanthropy24 Jan 2025

USAID funding withdrawn - Ghana programme and SL2 trial

Then, without warning, US foreign aid funding was withdrawn - closing the Ghana programme and halting the SL2 trial mid-stream. The shock sharpened the argument at the heart of James's moonshot philanthropy rather than settling it: no funder, government or philanthropist, is permanent - which is why his model asks philanthropy to do the things that outlast funding. Take the early risk. Build the evidence. Build what governments can own. The trials already completed, the UN resolution, the WHO targets - none of these could be unfunded. Work still on donor life-support could be, and was.

Clearly & advocacy3 Dec 2025

Clearly - second edition published

Eight years after the first edition, James returned to the book. What had been the case for a campaign was now a record of what the campaign achieved - the UN resolution, the evidence base, the WHO initiative - and an account of the way of giving that made it possible. Retitled A Short History of Eyeglasses and How a Moonshot Idea Can Change Everything, the second edition sets out James's journey as a blueprint others can use.

Research & evidence7 Apr 2026

PROSPER II published in the British Journal of Ophthalmology

The evidence keeps building. PROSPER II, a randomised controlled trial in Indian garment factories, extended the productivity findings beyond the tea fields into one of the world's largest and most structured industries - showing that glasses lift output on the factory floor too. James continues to back the trials that are changing how policymakers, governments and funders see uncorrected refractive error: not a health footnote, but the golden thread running through the development agenda.
